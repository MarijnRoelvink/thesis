Introduction

One of the global challenges we are facing currently is food insecurity: over 258 million people face acute food insecurity, requiring urgent food, nutrition and livelihood assistance. These numbers have been growing in the past years due to climate change, conflicts and soaring food prices due to COVID-19 and the war in Ukraine.

To which extent different countries are suffering from this condition is assessed by the Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC). These assessments are of high importance as they inform humanitarian organizations where they should send their aid to. Moreover, given the scarcity of humanitarian funds it is essential that these assessments are as accurate as possible: underestimating the problem results in unnecessary deaths and hardships, while overestimating it diverts limited humanitarian resources away from areas with greater needs.

The IPC utilizes an evidence-based consensus process to classify the extent of food insecurity within a country and identify the underlying factors driving it. This process relies on experts with extensive knowledge of the country's context and food security dynamics. This not only ensures that the results are backed up by causal factors that can be addressed, but also that the outcomes will be supported by sufficient important stakeholders within the country.

However, as building technical consensus takes a considerable amount of resources in terms of time and data gathering, this process is performed only once or twice a year. As such, this gives much room for FI situations to change in the meantime, making it hard for organizations to act on what the current FI situation actually demands. The IPC does make projections on what the FI situation will look like 3 to 6 months in the future, but these projections only work with the most-likely scenario and are based on assumptions that may not retain their validity throughout the time in between assessments. Therefore, we propose to develop a tool that monitors whether the predicted FI scenarios in-between FI analyses still hold by assessing whether the assumptions underlying the predictions remain valid. This tool would then serve as a warning system that could be used by the IPC to discover when an analysis needs to be redone to account for the changed circumstances. Moreover, by formalising the assumptions underlying the assessments, our tool could facilitate an unambiguous interpretation of the scenarios that could be used in different countries.

Given the increasing urgency of this problem and the availability of new data sources, there have been numerous attempts in recent years at using different forms of machine learning to predict Food Insecurity classifications for countries. These models have been developed in isolation from the IPC consensus process and would function as a replacement of the consensus process rather than an enhancement. However, given the high-risk and context-sensitive nature of food insecurity, we believe that it is not possible to replace expert assessments with a complete data-driven model and should not be strived for. Instead, we rather see the value of using modelling in clarifying and enhancing the current analysis process. Our research will therefore not solely be an opportunity-driven project where we develop a machine-learning model that predicts food insecurity, but a project that starts with investigating how the current process works and where a computer model could provide additional insights to the people leading this process. Following this line, we have chosen to structure our project according to human-centred design principles, where in each phase the feedback of its intended users is guiding for the further development of the model.
